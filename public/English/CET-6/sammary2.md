There is a growing trend among young people to look up to actors and singers as their role models. The trend itself need not alarm us, nor should it be lazily dismissed as proof of superficial values/This trend itself is not necessarily a problem, nor does it mean young people have superficial values.After all, admiration is a natural response to talent and success.However, it is crucial to recognize that the objects of this admiration are often carefully filtered and polished constructions designed for public consumption, rather than whole, authentic individuals.
Mass media can elevate a figure overnight and dismantle that same figure with equal speed, yet its greater talent lies in blurring the line between persona and person.By selectively highlighting virtues, concealing flaws, amplifying charm, and constructing a sense of intimacy, it invites audiences to mistake a carefully curated narrative for an authentic self.Yet it is precisely this illusion of seamlessness that makes such images so seductive, for they offer a temporary refuge from the disorder, friction, and incompleteness of ordinary life.Worse still, when such manufactured perfection is encountered again and again, repeated exposure gradually dulls our instinct for doubt/Over time, we lose the instinct to question the inherent unnaturalness of such seamless portrayals. More troubling than the appeal itself, however, is what follows: the slow erosion of our capacity for independent judgment.
结尾1：What is needed, then, is not a moralistic sermon against celebrity culture, but a more disciplined form of admiration. Young people must learn to distinguish talent from virtue, charisma from character, and public visibility from intrinsic worth.At a broader level, governments should strengthen regulation of digital platforms to prevent commercial interests from dominating the public cultural space. Meanwhile, schools and media institutions should cultivate media literacy and critical thinking, helping audiences see beyond carefully manufactured public images.
结尾2：What is needed, then, is not a moralistic sermon against celebrity culture, but a more disciplined form of admiration. Young people must learn to distinguish talent from virtue, charisma from character, and public visibility from intrinsic worth.Above all, they must remain anchored in their own lives. No idol, however luminous, will ever step down from the poster to answer one’s emails, sit one’s exams, or clear up the unfinished mess of ordinary life.
结尾3：What is needed, then, is not a moralistic sermon against celebrity culture, but a more disciplined form of admiration. Young people must learn to distinguish talent from virtue, charisma from character, and public visibility from intrinsic worth.Above all, they must remain anchored in their own lives.After all, true inspiration is drawn not from the flawless illusions projected on a screen, but from observing how flawed, real human beings navigate the unfinished mess of ordinary life.
